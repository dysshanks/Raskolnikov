# Code of Conduct

## I.

There is a question that has haunted every thinking person since the first man
raised his hand against another, since the first fire was stolen, since the first
boundary was drawn in the dirt with a stick: *Am I permitted?*

It is not a legal question. The law is a crude instrument, a blunt cudgel
wielded by the fearful against the daring. It shifts with the seasons, bends to
the whims of kings and committees, and has, on more occasions than any of us
would like to admit, been thoroughly, catastrophically wrong. No, the question
is deeper, older, more terrible. It lives in the bones. It visits you at three
in the morning when sleep will not come. It asks: *By what right do I act?*

Raskolnikov — the character, the idea, the warning — believed he had answered
this question with the finality of a man who has thought through every angle and
found the universe wanting. He believed there existed extraordinary men for whom
the ordinary laws of man did not apply. Men who could step across the line. Men
who could take, break, even kill, not because they *could*, but because they
*dared*. He wrote an essay about it. He published it. He convinced himself that
Napoleon would not have hesitated, that the great architects of history were not
bound by the petty moralities of the grocer and the clerk, and that he —
Rodion Romanovich Raskolnikov — was of that blood.

He was wrong.

Not a little wrong. Not wrong in the way a man is wrong about the weather. He
was wrong in the way a man is wrong when he has built an entire philosophy on a
foundation of sand, and the tide is coming in, and he cannot swim. The horror of
his discovery was not that he had been caught. The horror was that he could not
bear what he had done. The act itself — not its consequences, not the police,
not the interrogation, not the suspicion of his friends, but the *act* — broke
him. He found that he was not extraordinary at all. He was merely a man who had
done a terrible thing, and the weight of it was infinite, and no philosophy, no
theory, no carefully reasoned essay could lift it from his shoulders.

He confessed not because he was afraid of prison, but because he could no longer
live with himself. And that, perhaps, is the most terrible punishment of all:
the one we inflict on ourselves, the one from which there is no appeal.

## II.

Raskolnikov — the tool — is named in memory of his failure, not in celebration
of his ambition. Let us be clear about this, because names carry meaning, and
meaning carries weight, and weight, if you are not careful, will crush you.

The name is a reminder. Every time you run this software, you are invoking the
ghost of a man who thought he was above the law and discovered, in the most
agonising way possible, that he was not. You are standing in the shadow of his
mistake. The question is whether you will learn from it or repeat it.

This tool is designed for authorised security testing and CTF competitions. It
exists to help security professionals find vulnerabilities before they are
exploited, to strengthen systems, to protect the weak from those who would do
them harm. That is its purpose, its *raison d'être*. To use it otherwise is not
merely illegal — it is a philosophical error of the first order, a betrayal of
the very idea that gave it a name.

Consider: the name Raskolnikov comes from the Russian word *raskolnik*,
meaning "schism" or "split". The character is split — between his intellect and
his conscience, between his theory and his humanity, between the man he thinks
he is and the man he actually is. This tool, too, represents a split: the
dividing line between authorised and unauthorised, between the white hat and the
black, between the security researcher who makes the world safer and the
intruder who makes it more dangerous. Which side of that line you stand on is
entirely your choice. But choose you must. There is no neutral ground.

Every tool carries within it the intention of its maker and the character of its
user. A knife can heal or kill. Fire can warm or burn. Knowledge can free or
condemn. Raskolnikov is no different. It is a mirror held up to the operator,
and what it reflects is entirely your own. If you look into that mirror and see
nothing to fear, then you have not understood the name you are invoking.

## III.

If you find yourself wondering whether you should use this tool on a system you
do not own or do not have permission to test, pause. Sit with the question. Let
it breathe. Ask yourself not what you *can* do, but what you *should* do.

This is not a trivial distinction. The entire edifice of human morality rests
upon it. Every ethical system ever devised, from the Ten Commandments to Kant's
categorical imperative to the simple golden rule your grandmother taught you,
is an attempt to bridge the gap between capability and permissibility. We have
been trying to answer this question for ten thousand years. Do not assume you
can solve it in the time it takes to type a command.

The answer, if you are honest, will be clear. It will arrive not as a
thunderclap but as a quiet certainty, the way you know that a colour is red or
that a note is out of tune. You will know because you already know. You have
always known. The only question is whether you have the courage to admit it.

Do not use this tool illegally. Do not rationalise the transgression. Do not
convince yourself that you are the extraordinary man for whom the rules do not
apply. Do not tell yourself that you are just testing, just looking, just
curious. Do not whisper that no one will notice, that no harm will be done, that
the system is poorly configured anyway and someone else would find it first.
These are not arguments. They are excuses, and they are as old as sin itself.

Raskolnikov tried that path. He tried every rationalisation. He told himself the
pawnbroker was evil, that her money would do more good in his hands, that the
world would be better off without her, that he was not killing a person but a
principle. He was wrong about every single one of these things, and he knew it
even as he climbed the stairs with the axe hidden beneath his coat.

The path led him to a cramped room in St. Petersburg, to a fever dream, to the
slow, grinding terror of a man who cannot escape his own thoughts. It led him to
confession, to Siberia, to the slow, painful, decades-long reconstruction of a
soul he had shattered with his own hands.

You are not above the law. None of us are. That is not a limitation to be
overcome. It is not a weakness to be exploited. It is the foundation of any
society worth living in, the invisible architecture that separates civilisation
from the law of the jungle. Every time someone decides they are the exception,
that architecture weakens. Do not be the one who weakens it.

## IV.

Beyond the question of legality lies another question, perhaps even more
difficult: the question of how we treat one another.

Be excellent to one another. This is not a suggestion. It is not a nice-to-have.
It is the minimum viable requirement for any community that hopes to survive its
own disagreements.

Share knowledge freely. The security community has always been built on the
principle of open exchange — researchers publishing findings, developers sharing
code, practitioners teaching one another. This is not an accident. It is the
engine that drives the entire field forward. When you hoard knowledge, you do
not gain an advantage. You merely slow the progress of everyone, including
yourself.

Credit the work of others. Plagiarism is not merely unethical; it is a form of
theft, and it poisons the well for everyone. If you stand on the shoulders of
giants, at least have the decency to acknowledge that you did not get there on
your own.

Do not hoard discoveries that could protect someone's safety. If you find a
vulnerability, disclose it responsibly. Give the affected party time to respond.
But do not sit on critical findings indefinitely, and do not use them as
bargaining chips. People's security — sometimes people's lives — depend on
information being shared with those who need it.

Collapse the distinction between master and student. We are all learning, all
the time, until the very end. The moment you think you have nothing left to
learn is the moment you have stopped growing, and a community that stops growing
is a community that has begun to die. Teach what you know. Ask about what you do
not. Be wrong publicly, and learn from it. Be right humbly, and share the
credit.

Help those who ask. The person struggling with a concept today may be the person
who helps you tomorrow. Security is not a solo sport. None of us is smart enough
to hold all of this in our heads at once. We need each other. That is not a
weakness. It is the entire point.

Forgive those who err. Everyone makes mistakes. Everyone has gaps in their
knowledge. Everyone has said something stupid, done something careless, broken
something they should not have. The measure of a person is not whether they have
made mistakes but whether they have learned from them.

Build, do not destroy. The world is full of people who can break things. It is
much harder to build them, much harder to leave a place better than you found
it. Be one of the builders.

And when you close this terminal and walk away from the screen, carry with you
the understanding that what you have done here — what you *choose* to do — is
not merely a technical act. It is a moral one. You cannot escape that, no matter
how many layers of abstraction you pile between yourself and the consequence of
your keystrokes.

## V.

Let us speak now of Sonya.

In the novel, Sonya Marmeladova is the one who saves Raskolnikov — not through
cleverness, not through argument, not through force of will, but through quiet,
unwavering compassion. She is a prostitute, a social outcast, a person the world
has trampled underfoot. And yet she is the only one who can reach him. She reads
him the story of Lazarus, weeps with him, follows him to Siberia. She does not
lecture him. She does not condemn him. She simply stays.

The lesson — if there is a lesson — is this: the people who change the world are
not always the ones who seem powerful. Sometimes they are the ones who refuse to
look away from suffering. Sometimes they are the ones who stay when everyone
else has left.

The security community needs more Sonyas and fewer Raskolnikovs. It needs people
who protect the vulnerable, not people who rationalise exploiting them. It needs
people who stay, who help, who refuse to look away.

Be Sonya. Not Raskolnikov.

We named the tool after the one who failed so that you might remember not to.

## VI.

So: use Raskolnikov. Learn from it. Break things with it — in controlled
environments, with permission, with purpose. Strengthen the digital world one
vulnerability at a time. Write tools, share techniques, teach others, make the
world marginally less broken than you found it.

But never mistake the tool for the justification.

The justification must come from you.

And if you find yourself standing on the edge of a decision, unsure whether to
step forward or step back, do not ask what the tool can do. Ask what you can
live with.

Raskolnikov could not live with what he did. He spent the rest of his life
paying for a moment of arrogance. Do not make the same mistake. The world has
enough cautionary tales. It could use a few more people who learned from them.
